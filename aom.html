Architecture discipline — C1–C10

This AOM reflects Calmly platform costs only. Pool operator costs (solicitors, litigation, court filing, working capital for claims in flight) are excluded — they belong to the pool operator's P&L, not Calmly's.

Calmly is a verified dispute resolution marketplace. It does not own claims, bear litigation risk, employ solicitors, or fund court proceedings. The EV assessment app and pool insurance app are verified apps on the marketplace — curated by Calmly, delivered by independent third parties. Calmly earns platform fees from both parties per claim resolved, and marketplace fees from verified app providers.

  • Excluded: solicitor salaries, law student supervision, N1 court filing fees, pool insurance premiums (paid by pool members — Calmly earns marketplace fee from insurer, not the premium itself), working capital for claims in litigation, SRA compliance costs at the operating unit level.
  • Included: technology infrastructure, claim intake and eligibility assessment, independent EV assessment delivery (routed via independent assessment org under C3 — £16.28/claim), defendant onboarding and settlement facilitation, pool operator relationship management, pool insurance app curation (C4 — ~0.5 FTE HQ, ~£2/claim amortised), ITP outcomes reporting (C8 — quarterly + annual per active ITP, ~£4/claim amortised at scale), assessment org pool QA framework (C9 — multi-org structure, HQ function), claim data infrastructure and EV accuracy tracking (C10 — HQ fixed infrastructure, strategic asset management).
  • Not modelled (requires later architecture): securitisation vehicle management, SPV operations, investor relations infrastructure at institutional scale, AML compliance for investor onboarding. These are flagged [REQUIRES later architecture] and excluded from cost lines.
Governing thought

Calmly is not a law firm operating at scale. It is the market infrastructure that makes the small claims litigation market function — the exchange, the rating methodology, the settlement facilitation layer. The pool operator brings the legal machinery; Calmly provides the credible, independent signal that makes rational settlement possible on both sides.

The operating model is designed around that role. The last-mile unit is a Calmly platform team, not a student law clinic. The binding cost is Calmly's own staff time for intake, EV assessment delivery, defendant engagement, and settlement facilitation — not solicitor supervision or court filing. The exchange earns fees from both parties because it delivers real value to both: the claimant gets a credible, independently assessed offer; the defendant gets a bounded, verifiable liability and a structured path to closure.

Last-mile unit (LMU)

Platform team

Structural bound
One LMU per county court catchment area. The county court defines the LMU boundary: all claims within a catchment file at the same court, jurisdiction is resolved by definition, and pool operators know exactly where litigation will proceed. The LMU boundary is geographic and externally defined — not a function of team headcount or throughput assumptions.
Headcount
4–5 — 1 intake and eligibility assessor, 1 EV assessment specialist, 1 defendant engagement specialist, 1 settlement facilitator (roles may overlap at lower volume). Capacity
Determined by claim volume arising within the county court catchment. No dedicated premises required. Platform work is fully remote via the exchange system. Video calls for claimant and defendant engagement.
ITP supply chain

Section 3

Platform team design — the last-mile unit

The platform team is designed right-to-left: starting from the point of customer contact (Using stream) and working back through Making. The pool operator's work sits outside this design entirely — it begins after WP3 (pool matching) and produces no cost lines on Calmly's P&L.

Section 5

Revenue model — platform fees from both parties

Calmly earns platform fees from both the claimant and the defendant per claim resolved. Fees are grounded in the C2 price ceiling analysis — the maximum fee each party would pay before it becomes cheaper to revert to their existing routine. Both fees are contingent on resolution: neither party pays unless the claim is settled or adjudicated.

Claimant-side fee

Platform fee from claimant

Trigger: claim resolved (settled or adjudicated).

Basis: percentage of settlement value received, deducted at settlement. Claimant pays from money they did not previously have — no budget line problem (C6).

Price ceiling (C2a): the cost the claimant avoids is the full cost of legal escalation — up to £1,850 in legal support + court costs. Calmly's fee must be below this to deliver a net positive for the claimant. At a £1,500 average claim, a 20% fee = £300 — materially below the alternative cost ceiling.

Indicative fee range: 15–25% of settlement value received.

TIER 4 ASSUMPTION: 20% mid-point. Fee ceiling analysis from C2a. No empirical pricing data for this market exists. Comparator: Resolver commercial tier (flat fee); US contingency fee norm (25–40%, but in a recoverable-costs market).
Defendant-side fee

Platform fee from defendant

Trigger: buyout agreement signed at D3 (legal closure). Calmly's fee is earned at the point of legal closure — not contingent on D4 repayment, which is between the defendant and the buyout operator.

Basis: fixed platform fee per claim resolved, earned for defendant routing and buyout app curation. Defendant pays for: (a) the bounded, independently assessed liability (C2b); (b) access to the defendant liability buyout app that extinguishes legal exposure at D3; (c) structured facilitation through the buyout decision. The buyout operator operates on their own P&L — Calmly earns a curation fee for routing defendants to the app, not a share of the repayment stream.

Price ceiling (C2b): the defendant avoids the cost of uncertainty — estimated at up to £1,850 in legal fees plus the psychological and reputational cost of open-ended exposure. The buyout adds a further value dimension: legal closure at D3 (vs remaining in a live dispute during repayment under the old financing app design). The certainty of legal closure is worth paying for.

Indicative fee range: £150–£300 flat per claim resolved, or 5–10% of settlement amount.

TIER 4 ASSUMPTION: £200 flat fee mid-point (or 7% of settlement). Grounded in C2b price ceiling. The buyout design (legal closure at D3) increases the value delivered to the defendant vs the old financing app (legal dispute open during repayment) — the fee ceiling may be higher under the new design. Validate at C6 design stage. Note: the platform fee is Calmly's revenue for routing; the buyout operator's margin on the repayment stream is separate and off Calmly's P&L.
v2.0 C6 update: Defendant-side fee trigger updated from "claim resolved (settled or adjudicated)" to "buyout agreement signed at D3". Calmly earns a platform routing and app curation fee — not a share of the defendant's repayment to the buyout operator. The buyout operator's P&L is independent. Fee ceiling analysis is unchanged; if anything, it is higher because legal closure at D3 is a more complete value delivery than the old financing app's settlement-with-open-litigation design.
Revenue per claim (illustrative, TIER 4): At £1,500 average claim value, 70% settled before court:
  • Claimant fee: 20% × £1,500 × [claimant's recovered amount] — assume 80% EV recovery → claimant receives ~£1,200; Calmly earns ~£240
  • Defendant fee: £200 flat per resolved claim
  • Gross platform revenue per resolved claim: ~£440
  • Per-claim revenue across full funnel (including non-settling claims): £440 × 70% settle rate + £0 × 30% court rate ≈ £308 blended
TIER 4 ASSUMPTION: All revenue figures are illustrative. Both fee rates and settlement amounts require empirical validation. The fin-sim will run sensitivity analysis across fee rate × average claim value × settlement rate. The key FMOS driver is the ratio of per-claim revenue to per-claim platform cost — not the absolute fee level.
