Section 1 — Strategic design: R8 Switching Cost

Key Store of Value

Extended product use routine: After one resolved claim, the consumer has a verified record of a successfully recovered amount, an established relationship with an institution that acted on their behalf, and a documented evidence trail within the Calmly platform.

Store of value type: Relationship capital (trust + verified outcome record) + data accumulation (claim history, evidence patterns, established assessment org familiarity with their case type).

Important caveat: Individual consumers rarely have multiple disputes within a short period. The switching cost is strongest for specific high-frequency segments: tenants (landlord deposit disputes are common and recurring), small businesses using Calmly for B2C disputes, and consumers who become advocates. The model should not assume general population multi-claim behaviour.

Theory of Change — Lock-in (CMO format)

Class of problem: Switching cost — the consumer's accumulated history and relationship capital are not portable to a competitor.

Theory: Klemperer (1987) switching cost theory — accumulated relationship capital makes switching costly because alternatives start from zero. Luhmann (1979) institutional trust — trust built through repeated positive interactions is not transferable. Both are well-grounded frameworks in economics and sociology respectively.

Context: Consumer with one or more resolved claims who faces a new dispute.

Mechanism: When a new dispute arises, the consumer's established claim history (evidence of prior successful recovery, established relationship with the assessment org, documented company response patterns) means their next claim is faster and lower friction than starting fresh elsewhere. The switching cost is the loss of this accumulated knowledge — competitors require starting the credibility-building process from scratch.

Outcome: Multi-claim consumers have a structural preference for Calmly. Returning claimants have lower effective CAC (zero new acquisition spend) and lower intake cost (established evidence patterns reduce intake assessor time).

Switching cost mechanisms by segment

Tenants (primary segment): Landlord deposit disputes are recurring (new tenancy every 1–3 years). Established evidence patterns from prior disputes + assessment org familiarity = fast-track second claim. Switching cost: loss of established track record.

Small businesses (secondary segment): Repeated B2C disputes with different consumers follow similar patterns. Platform claim history enables pattern matching that accelerates processing. Switching cost: loss of portfolio data.

Advocates (tertiary): Consumers who become advocates — their identity as "someone who beat a company through Calmly" has social value. Referral network is a form of relationship capital. Switching cost: loss of social proof story tied to specific brand.

One-time consumers (general population): No meaningful switching cost. The R8 mechanism is weakest here — this is flagged as a model limitation for the fin-sim CLV assumptions.

R8 value not captured in FMOS (model limitation)

The per-claim FMOS calculation does not capture R8's value. The switching cost mechanism raises customer lifetime value (CLV) for repeat-use segments (tenants, small businesses, advocates). A returning claimant has: zero CAC (no acquisition spend), reduced intake time (established evidence patterns), and higher acceptance probability (prior positive experience). These benefits compound the unit economics but are not visible in a single-claim cost floor model. The fin-sim CLV assumptions must capture this — flagged as a condition.

Section 3 — I: Intrinsic performance capability
Pass

The switching cost mechanism has a compounding property. Each resolved claim increases the returning claimant's value: zero CAC on return, faster intake (evidence patterns established), and higher advocacy probability (positive outcomes generate referrals). As the consumer base grows, the proportion of returning claimants and advocates increases — effectively reducing the average CAC across the portfolio without additional investment. This is a structural property of the architecture. Pass.

Section 4 — T: Theorem-driven design
Pass

Lock-in — Klemperer (1987) switching cost theory

Klemperer's switching cost theory is a foundational result in industrial organisation — accumulated relationship capital creates lock-in when alternatives require rebuilding from zero. Widely applied in financial services (bank account switching costs), software (data portability), and professional services (accountant/lawyer relationship capital). The application here is direct: claim history and assessment org familiarity are not portable. Pass.

Trust accumulation — Luhmann (1979)

Institutional trust is built through repeated positive interactions and is not transferable to competitors. A consumer who has experienced a successful resolution has formed a trust relationship that a new entrant cannot inherit. Well-grounded in sociology and widely applied in service industry research. Pass.

Section 5 — Verdict
Verdict — R8 gate · 19 May 2026
PASS

FMOS: 66% — unchanged from R7. R8 adds no new per-unit cost.

The switching cost mechanism is architecturally embedded — claim history accumulates as a byproduct of operations. The per-claim FMOS model understates R8's value: CLV improves for repeat-use segments (tenants, small businesses) through zero CAC on return and reduced intake time. This must be modelled in the fin-sim CLV assumptions.

Important flag: the switching cost is weakest for the general consumer population (infrequent disputes). The mechanism is strongest for tenants and small businesses. The fin-sim should weight repeat-use segments appropriately.

Proceed to R9 — Circumvent Supplier Leverage.

Section 6 — Conditions before capital commitment
  1. Condition 1Model CLV by segment in fin-sim. The current fin-sim models one claim per consumer. Add repeat-use segments: tenants (assumed 2.5 claims over 5 years), small businesses (assumed 5 claims/year). Model the CAC and intake cost savings on return claims. This will improve the fin-sim's at-scale revenue projections without changing the per-claim cost floor.
  2. Condition 2Instrument claim history access in the platform. The switching cost requires consumers to have easy access to their claim history. The platform must surface prior claim context when a returning consumer begins a new submission — not require them to re-enter all evidence. This is a product design requirement for the platform.
