Section 1 — Strategic design: R5 Use Block

Use Block diagnosis

Primary block: Evidence assembly anxiety. The consumer does not know what evidence is needed, does not know if they have enough, and fears that submitting an incomplete case will weaken their position or waste time.

Key clarification: In Calmly's exchange model, the consumer is not submitting to court — they are submitting to Calmly. The activation question is whether the consumer can submit dispute details in a way that gives the assessment org sufficient input to produce a valid EV report.

Secondary block: Intimidation from perceived legal complexity. Consumers associate claim submission with legal form-filling (County Court Money Claims Centre) — a genuinely complex process. They must understand that Calmly's submission is not a court filing.

Theory of Change — Adoption (CMO format)

Class of problem: Competence gap + completion anxiety — consumers do not know what "good enough" looks like and fear failure.

Theory: Bandura (1986) self-efficacy — belief in one's ability to complete a task determines whether one attempts it and persists through difficulty. Guided structured input with explicit evidence standards reduces perceived competence gap and raises task completion probability. Thaler & Sunstein (2008) choice architecture — defaults and progress indicators guide behaviour toward completion without removing agency.

Context: Consumer who has accepted in principle (R4 PASS) but faces the evidence submission step with a pile of emails, screenshots, and receipts and no sense of what matters.

Mechanism: A structured claim submission flow with progressive disclosure (step-by-step, showing one category at a time), in-context evidence standards (examples of sufficient evidence for each claim type), and a live completeness indicator produces self-efficacy — consumer sees they already have enough to proceed.

Outcome: Consumer completes claim submission with all required evidence. Assessment org receives sufficient input to produce EV assessment within 48 hours.

Adoption Strategy

Guided claim submission flow with progressive disclosure and real-time completeness feedback. The platform shows the consumer: (1) exactly which categories of evidence are needed for their specific claim type, (2) examples of sufficient evidence at each step, (3) a live completeness indicator confirming when they have enough. No legal jargon. Auto-save prevents loss of progress. The consumer is told whether they have a complete submission before submitting — eliminating fear of an incomplete case and reducing support burden.

Cross-requirement consistency

The R5 guided submission flow is also the R3 assessment org input quality mechanism. The evidence categories the flow collects are designed around what the assessment org requires to produce a valid EV report. R5 and R3 share a single designed artefact: the claim intake form architecture. This is not incidental — the two requirements are structurally linked through the assessment org's input specification.

Section 3 — I: Intrinsic performance capability
Pass

The guided submission flow has two intrinsic performance properties. First, it reduces drop-off during submission — fewer incomplete submissions means more claims reach the assessment org, which means more assessments per unit of fixed content/acquisition cost. This improves the effective CAC (more completions per £ of acquisition spend). Second, higher evidence quality at intake reduces back-and-forth between the assessment org and the claimant, cutting the LMU specialist intake time (currently 69 min/confirmed claim). Both effects reduce cost per resolved claim over time without requiring additional investment. Pass.

Section 4 — T: Theorem-driven design
Pass

Adoption — Bandura (1986) self-efficacy

Perceived competence is the primary determinant of task initiation and completion. Bandura's self-efficacy theory is one of the most replicated findings in behavioural psychology — applied extensively in digital product design (onboarding flows, step-by-step guidance, progress indicators). The specific mechanism — guided structured input with explicit standards — directly reduces perceived competence gap. Applicable precedent: financial services (open banking claim flows), legal tech (Resolver's structured complaint submission), consumer platforms (insurance claim flows). Pass.

Choice architecture — Thaler & Sunstein (2008)

Progressive disclosure (showing one step at a time) and a live completeness indicator are choice architecture interventions that reduce cognitive load and nudge toward completion. Thaler & Sunstein's nudge framework is well-grounded and extensively applied in digital consumer products. Pass.

Section 6 — Conditions before capital commitment
  1. Condition 1Validate evidence category requirements with the assessment org. The guided submission flow must be built around the specific evidence categories the assessment org needs. This requires a detailed intake specification from the assessment org before the product is built. Dependency: assessment org partnership confirmed (C3 condition).
  2. Condition 2Measure submission completion rate in pilot. Target: ≥75% of consumers who begin submission complete it. If below 60%, the flow needs redesign before scaling. Pilot must instrument completion at each step to identify drop-off points.
  3. Condition 3Validate LMU intake time reduction. If the guided flow improves evidence quality, intake assessor time should fall below the 69 min/confirmed claim baseline. Track in pilot — every 10-minute reduction saves ~£5/claim on the RC layer.
