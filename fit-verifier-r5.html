Section 1 — Strategic design: R5 Use Block

Use Block diagnosis

Primary block: anticipated litigation complexity (steps 5–6 transition). The consumer, having formed the intention to join the pool (C4 resolved), carries a mental model of what being a party to litigation requires: court hearings, CPR compliance, legal document review, tactical decisions. This expectation of sustained specialist-grade effort is the Use Block. It manifests at sign-up — consumers hesitate to commit, anticipating ongoing demands, or disengage post-sign-up when they expect to be called upon to act.

Secondary block: evidence assembly anxiety (step 2). The consumer does not know what evidence is needed, does not know if they have enough, and fears that submitting an incomplete case will weaken their position or waste time. This block is bounded — it applies to a single, one-time submission step — and is addressed by the guided intake flow.

Theory of Change — Adoption (CMO format)

Class of problem: Transaction cost mismatch — lay claimants bear prohibitively high transaction costs when managing CPR-compliant litigation; a professional pool operator bears low transaction costs for the same activity. The consumer's use block is not a perception problem — it is a rational response to a genuine cost disparity.

Theory: Coase (1937), "The Nature of the Firm" — economic activities are organised by the party that can execute them at lowest total cost. Williamson (1985) — asset specificity: when one party holds specialist assets (legal expertise, CPR infrastructure, court familiarity, economies of scale across a portfolio of claims), assigning the activity to that party minimises total transaction costs and eliminates the cost imposed on the party without those assets.

Context: Consumer who has signed the pool participation agreement and anticipates being required to manage CPR-compliant litigation as a lay claimant — with no legal training, no court familiarity, and no economies of scale.

Mechanism: Pool operator holds all post-submission litigation management by architectural design — CPR compliance, evidence bundle preparation, court filing, defendant correspondence, judgment enforcement. Consumer's required actions after sign-up: receive notifications, receive payment. No court attendance. No CPR compliance. No legal document review. The consumer's use routine is bounded to one guided submission session and passive outcome receipt.

Outcome: Consumer's anticipated litigation complexity is eliminated — not reduced, eliminated — because the consumer is not a party to the litigation management activity. Adoption disruption is bounded to the guided submission step only.

Adoption Strategy

Primary move — Coase assignment: assign all post-submission litigation management to the pool operator as professional litigant. This is not a service tier or an optional upgrade — it is the structural default of pool membership. Calmly communicates the consumer's passive role explicitly and early: at pool invitation, at sign-up, and in the participation agreement. The consumer knows before committing what they will and will not be required to do.

Secondary move — guided submission flow: addresses the secondary block (evidence assembly anxiety at step 2). Progressive disclosure, in-context evidence standards, and a live completeness indicator remove the fear of an incomplete submission. Note: the guided submission flow also improves assessment org input quality (C3) — same designed artefact, two requirements served.

Section 3 — I: Intrinsic performance capability
Pass

The professional litigant structure has an intrinsic performance property independent of adoption. The pool operator manages litigation at scale — across a portfolio of claims — with economies of scale unavailable to any individual lay claimant. This drives higher settlement rates and better per-claim outcomes than self-represented litigation, which improves per-claim revenue and strengthens the pool's contingent fee yield. The Coase assignment is therefore not merely a use block solution; it is a performance architecture.

The guided submission flow has a second intrinsic performance property: higher evidence quality at intake reduces back-and-forth between the assessment org and the claimant, cutting LMU specialist intake time (currently ~69 min/confirmed claim). Both effects reduce cost per resolved claim over time without requiring additional investment. Pass.

Section 4 — T: Theorem-driven design
Pass

Primary — Coase (1937), "The Nature of the Firm"

Coase's central insight: economic activities are organised by the party that can execute them at lowest total cost. Where one party has lower transaction costs for an activity, assigning that activity to them is allocatively efficient. Applied here: CPR-compliant litigation management has near-zero transaction cost for a professional pool operator (specialist assets, established process, economies of scale) and prohibitively high transaction cost for a lay claimant (no legal training, no court familiarity, no scale). The efficient assignment is the one the architecture makes: pool operator manages all post-submission litigation. This is not a design choice — it is the economically necessary allocation. Pass.

Secondary — Williamson (1985), transaction cost economics and asset specificity

Williamson extends Coase: when one party holds highly specific assets — expertise, infrastructure, relationships — that cannot easily be replicated by the other party, governance structures that assign activities to the asset holder minimise total transaction costs. The pool operator holds specific legal assets (CPR expertise, court familiarity, solicitor relationships, claims portfolio management infrastructure) that the lay claimant cannot acquire for a single claim. Assigning litigation management to the pool operator is therefore not only efficient but structurally stable — it exploits genuine comparative advantage. Pass.

Secondary — Bandura (1986) self-efficacy (guided submission flow)

The guided submission flow retains Bandura's self-efficacy mechanism for the secondary block: evidence assembly anxiety at step 2. Perceived competence is the primary determinant of task initiation and completion. Structured input with explicit evidence standards and a live completeness indicator reduces the perceived competence gap, raising submission completion probability. This is a contained application — it addresses one bounded step — and does not bear the weight of the primary adoption strategy. Pass (secondary).

Section 5 — Verdict
Verdict — R5 gate · 19 May 2026
PASS

FMOS: 63.2% — unchanged from R4. R5 adds no new per-unit cost. The professional litigant structure is not a Calmly cost — the pool operator bears all litigation costs as part of its own P&L.

The Coase assignment eliminates the primary use block structurally — not by lowering the barrier but by removing the consumer from the activity entirely. The consumer's required actions after sign-up are: receive notifications, receive payment. There is no litigation management task for them to perform. This is also an architectural requirement of the pool model: the pool cannot let individual consumers manage their own litigation within a coordinated claims portfolio without undermining the pool's ability to run a coherent CPR-compliant strategy across claims.

The guided submission flow addresses the secondary block (evidence assembly anxiety) and also improves assessment org input quality — a single designed artefact serving R5 and C3. Consistent with the cross-requirement structure of the architecture.

Section 6 — Conditions before capital commitment
  1. Condition 1Validate consumer role communication. The Coase mechanism only holds if consumers understand their post-sign-up role before they sign up. The pilot must measure whether consumers have accurate expectations of what pool membership requires. Target: ≥80% of pool participants correctly understand (unprompted) that they will not need to attend court or manage legal documents, measured at 30-day post-sign-up check-in. If below 80%, communication architecture requires redesign before scaling.
  2. Condition 2Confirm pool operator contractual obligation to manage all litigation. The Coase assignment is an architectural claim — it must be contractually enforced. Pool membership agreements must assign all post-submission litigation management to the pool operator with no carve-outs that push tasks back to consumers. Validate: pool participation agreement reviewed by an SRA-registered solicitor before launch. Any carve-out that requires consumer action (beyond notification receipt) must be flagged as an architecture break and returned to design.
  3. Condition 3Validate guided submission completion rate. The secondary block mechanism requires the guided submission flow to achieve sufficient completion. Target: ≥75% of consumers who begin a submission complete it. Track drop-off by step in pilot. If overall completion falls below 60%, or if a single step accounts for >20% drop-off, that step requires redesign before scaling.
