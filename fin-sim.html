v2.0 architecture change — exchange model. This simulation models Calmly as a platform operator, not a claim purchaser. Calmly earns a platform fee per resolved transaction. The investor vehicle (pool operator) purchases claims, holds them, and earns the settlement spread — bearing all litigation risk. Calmly's cost base is exchange infrastructure and claim-processing operations, not working capital for claim acquisition. Compared with v2, the working capital requirement falls to near zero for Calmly; the required price is now a platform fee denominated in £ per resolved transaction (or % of settlement value).

Revenue model

Calmly earns a platform fee on each resolved transaction. It does not earn the settlement spread — that accrues to the investor vehicle. The fee is the price for originating the claim, processing the pre-trial bundle, facilitating the exchange listing, and delivering the administrative infrastructure that brings the claim to resolution.

Total annual revenue = Platform fee × claims resolved per year

Group 6 — Special mechanism: independent assessment org (C3)

The independent assessment org is not just a cost line — it is the architectural mechanism that improves the IC position. By foreclosing the defendant's credibility objection in mediation, it shortens recovery time from a weighted average of ~8.1 months (no mechanism) to ~4.65 months (mechanism active). This halves the peak WC commitment on assessment org payments from ~£4.3M to ~£2.2M at Y5 scale. The net economic effect: PVC increases by £16.28/claim, IC falls by ~£0.95/claim vs the no-mechanism baseline, and the at-scale pre-trial settlement rate improves from ~40% to ~70% — affecting revenue certainty, not cost directly.

Unit economics — by utilisation

Three scenarios: 50%, 75%, and 100% module utilisation. Revenue (the platform fee) is constant across all three — the required fee is set for the at-scale, 100% case. What changes is the fixed cost per claim, which determines whether the venture is making a profit or a loss at partial utilisation.

Important note on revenue per claim in this model: Calmly's revenue is the platform fee it charges per resolved transaction — not the settlement value or the investor's spread. The required fee below is derived by working backwards from total costs and the FMOS target. A fee set for 100% utilisation will produce a surplus at partial utilisation only if the variable cost per claim is low enough that the fixed cost shortfall is covered by the fee at lower volume. Why 30% FMOS?

The FMOS target of 30% is the IVE methodology default for a new market-creating venture. It reflects three requirements: (1) the venture must be sufficiently profitable that a management team would rationally choose to operate it over alternatives; (2) it must generate enough surplus to fund the investment return required by whoever backed the infrastructure build; (3) it must have enough margin buffer to absorb unfavourable movements in key assumptions — particularly the court win rate and capacity utilisation. At 30% FMOS, the venture survives a 10pp drop in win rate or a 15pp drop in utilisation without moving into loss. This is the margin of safety built into the required price.

Required price calculation

The required fee is the platform fee (£ per resolved claim) that produces a 30% FMOS at the at-scale, 100% utilisation case. The derivation is a single formula: total whole costs per claim ÷ (1 − FMOS target).

Sensitivity analysis

How does the required fee change when key assumptions move? Two sensitivity tables: court win rate (Tier 3 — the most credible input, also the most consequential) and average disputed amount (Tier 4 — the input with the highest uncertainty). The base case row is highlighted.

Sensitivity 1 — court win rate

Win rate affects the investor vehicle's return, not Calmly's platform fee directly — unless the fee is structured as a percentage of settlement. In the current £-fee model, a lower win rate reduces average settlement values (claims that go to court and lose generate no settlement). This affects the % of settlement metric but not the absolute £ required fee. The table below shows both the absolute fee and the % of settlement at each win rate scenario.

Sensitivity 2 — average disputed amount

The average disputed amount affects the absolute settlement value, the investor's return, and (if the fee were % of settlement) Calmly's revenue. In the £-fee model, it directly affects the % of settlement metric. If the claims entering the exchange skew lower than £3,000 (e.g. more £500–£1,500 claims), the fixed fee becomes a larger percentage of each settlement — which may affect claimant and investor willingness to use the exchange.
