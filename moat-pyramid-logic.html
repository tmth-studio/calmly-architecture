Calmly · Pyramid logic diagram · Minto Stage 1

Competitive position at scale — argument structure

21 May 2026 · Ordering principle applied below
SCQA
Situation
Calmly is a civil enforcement marketplace for unenforceable small claims in England & Wales. At scale, its financial model produces 62.3% FMOS on a £245/claim cost floor — a margin that rests entirely on the durability of its competitive position.
Complication
Marketplace businesses routinely claim network effects and moats that erode under competition. The financial model is only credible if the competitive position is structurally genuine — not a first-mover advantage that any well-resourced entrant can replicate with time and capital.
Question
Does Calmly's architecture produce a competitive moat that survives serious competitive pressure at scale?
Answer
Two wide moat sources compound over 8–12 years, but both depend on multi-category volume depth — making launch sequencing the critical strategic variable, not total claim volume.
Governing thought

Two wide moat sources compound over 8–12 years, but both depend on multi-category volume depth — making launch sequencing the critical strategic variable, not total claim volume.

Argument 1
The industry structure creates a durable first-mover window — incumbents cannot enter without abandoning their existing models
  • No commercial operator currently facilitates small claim settlement in England & Wales. The county court's cost-recovery rules make it structurally unprofitable for any solicitor-based model.
  • CAB, Resolver, and ombudsman schemes are not substitutes — they lack the pooling mechanism and EV methodology that constitute the new cost-benefit architecture (CBA).
  • The window is time-limited: it closes once Calmly has demonstrated the economics, attracting well-resourced imitators with capital to replicate the architecture.
Argument 2
The ITP switching cost stack is wide and self-deepening — no deliberate investment required to grow it
  • Burnham, Frels & Mahajan (2003) taxonomy: procedural (staff retraining, policy redrafting), financial (loss of the ITP-specific outcomes dataset cited in funder reports), relational (community reputation among members).
  • The outcomes dataset is load-bearing in ITP board and funder communications — switching creates an immediate, visible gap in institutional reporting.
  • Costs are non-transferable, non-purchasable, and compound nonlinearly with tenure. A competitor cannot inherit or accelerate them.
Argument 3
The EV accuracy data flywheel meets all three conditions for durable network effects — but its local nature is the architecture's single structural vulnerability
  • Dierickx & Cool (1989) time compression diseconomies confirmed: five years of calibration cannot be replicated in one year with more capital. The dataset requires actual claims in real time.
  • C6 buyout mechanism enriches the flywheel: defendant D1 response patterns add a second data dimension beyond settlement/not.
  • Local vulnerability: a vertical specialist focused on housing disputes for five years could build tighter within-category EV calibration than Calmly's mixed-category dataset — then attack the housing ITP relationship on outcome quality.
Argument 4
ROIC widens for 8–12 years then moderates — the financial model's CAP assumption is supportable but not indefinite
  • Four drivers move in the same direction in years 1–8: fixed cost amortisation; EV accuracy improvement (higher fee yield); assessment org fee compression (£16 → £10–12/claim at multi-org competition); ITP channel reducing blended CAC.
  • Widening moderates at year 8–12 as AI approximation narrows the EV methodology advantage and well-resourced imitators emerge with their own data.
  • The central CAP of 8–12 years is consistent with the £189M net exit in the financial model. When methodology becomes replicable, the moat rests on the ITP network and the data flywheel — both still wide at that point.
Ordering principle: structural order — MECE parts of a complete competitive position assessment (industry structure → demand-side switching costs → demand-side network effects → ROIC/CAP implications)
