Section 1 — Strategic design: R6 Cash Flow Constraint

Cash flow constraint diagnosis

Clarification — the consumer is not paying cash. In Calmly's exchange model, the "payment" is the discount the consumer must accept relative to the face value of their claim. Calmly offers a settlement amount lower than full face value in exchange for immediate, certain recovery.

The constraint: The consumer anchors on face value (what they are owed) and perceives Calmly's offer as "less than I deserve" — even when the expected value of the alternative (court, 12+ months, uncertain) is lower than Calmly's offer. This is loss aversion combined with a reference point problem.

The alternative the consumer faces: pursue the claim independently (expensive, time-consuming, uncertain) or give up entirely. Both alternatives have lower expected value than Calmly's offer. But the consumer's mental account anchors on face value, not expected value.

Theory of Change — Amortisation (CMO format)

Class of problem: Temporal discounting + loss aversion. The consumer weights a certain small loss (accepting discount vs face value) more heavily than an uncertain larger gain (court outcome).

Theory: Kahneman & Tversky (1979) Prospect Theory — value is assessed relative to a reference point, and losses loom larger than equivalent gains. Reframing shifts the reference point from face value to expected value, changing the perceived loss. Thaler (1980) mental accounting — the correct comparison is not face value vs Calmly offer, but expected value of alternatives vs Calmly offer.

Context: Consumer who has received Calmly's specific offer and is mentally comparing it to what they are owed (face value).

Mechanism: Presenting the offer relative to the claim's independently-assessed EV (not its face value) shifts the reference point. The independent EV assessment (already produced at R4) arrives before Calmly's offer, establishing EV as the frame. Calmly's offer is then presented as "Y% of your claim's expected value" — a gain framing — rather than "Y% less than what you're owed" — a loss framing.

Outcome: Consumer accepts the offer because the comparison is EV (independent assessment) vs Calmly offer, not face value vs Calmly offer.

Amortisation Strategy

The EV assessment frame is the amortisation mechanism. The independent assessment org's report establishes expected value — not face value — as the reference point before Calmly makes its offer. Calmly's offer is presented as a percentage of independently-assessed EV. The framing is architecturally sequenced: EV report arrives first, then offer. This is not a communication trick — the EV assessment is a genuine independent calculation, making the reference point credible and resistant to motivated reasoning.

Architectural elegance: one mechanism, three requirements

The C3 independent assessment org produces a single EV report per claim. That report serves:

C3: Third-party credibility signal for defendants and investors (shortens recovery time).

R4: Free conviction mechanism for potential claimants (dissolves credibility doubt about the model).

R6: Reference point anchor for the offer (shifts consumer comparison from face value to EV).

One mechanism, three problems solved. No additional cost or institutional actor required for R6. The amortisation strategy is built into the submission-to-offer sequence as a design property.

Section 3 — I: Intrinsic performance capability
Pass

The EV frame is produced by the same assessment org process that runs for every claim regardless of R6. There is no additional cost to produce the framing. As claim volume grows and EV assessment accuracy improves (R10 data flywheel), the offer-to-acceptance rate should improve — the EV frame becomes more credible as the reference data set behind it grows. This is an intrinsic improving mechanism: the more claims Calmly processes, the more accurate the EV reports, and the stronger the amortisation frame. Pass.

Section 4 — T: Theorem-driven design
Pass

Amortisation — Kahneman & Tversky (1979) Prospect Theory

Prospect Theory is one of the most replicated and robust findings in behavioural economics — Nobel Prize (Kahneman, 2002). The specific prediction — that losses loom larger than gains and reference points determine perceived value — is well-established. The mechanism used here (reframing by shifting reference point from face value to expected value) is a direct application. Extensive precedent in financial services and insurance offer design. Pass.

Mental accounting — Thaler (1980)

Thaler's mental accounting framework establishes that the consumer is making the wrong comparison (face value vs offer) rather than the right one (expected value of alternatives vs offer). The EV assessment corrects the comparison object before the offer arrives. Pass.
