Section 1 — Strategic design: R10 Competitive Moat

Moat diagnosis

What makes Calmly structurally hard to replicate after 3–4 years of at-scale operation?

Primary moat — data flywheel (compound accuracy advantage): Every resolved claim adds to Calmly's dataset of defendant response patterns, claim EV accuracy, and settlement timelines. This improves the assessment org's EV accuracy over time. A new entrant starts with no data and therefore produces inferior EV assessments → inferior pool operator pricing → smaller margin → weaker economics. The moat widens with volume.

Secondary moat — methodology ownership: The EV assessment methodology, quality standards, and training framework are Calmly's IP. Not patentable, but 2–3 years to calibrate against real case outcomes. A competitor must build this from scratch.

Tertiary moat — assessment org network: Each onboarded university law department has invested in training and calibration. They are sticky partners (switching to a competitor requires re-training). A competitor must build this network simultaneously with building volume — a difficult chicken-and-egg problem.

Quaternary moat — pool operator calibration: Pool operators have invested in understanding Calmly's claim portfolio quality. Their pricing models are calibrated to Calmly's EV accuracy. Switching platforms requires recalibrating the entire pricing framework — a significant switching cost for operators.

Theory of Change — Moat (CMO format)

Class of problem: Structural competitive advantage — creating barriers that prevent replication even when the idea is visible.

Theory: Metcalfe (1980) network effects — value grows with nodes (claim data, assessment org connections, pool operator relationships). Arrow (1962) learning-by-doing — EV assessment accuracy improves with cumulative claim volume (a manufacturing learning curve applied to an information product). Teece (1986) complementary assets — proprietary methodology + data + relationships are complementary assets that together create durable advantage; individually replicable, together extremely difficult to replicate simultaneously.

Mechanism: As claim volume grows, EV accuracy improves → pool operators price more accurately (tighter spread required) → Calmly can offer claimants better deals → more claimants choose Calmly → more volume → more accuracy. This is a positive feedback loop that widens the moat over time.

Outcome: After 36+ months of at-scale operation, a new entrant cannot replicate Calmly's EV accuracy without the historical data — and cannot build historical data without the volume that the accuracy advantage enables. The moat is self-reinforcing.

Leverage Strategy

Calmly's leverage at scale: data quality as a pricing advantage. Pool operators price claim pools based on EV accuracy — the tighter the confidence interval on expected settlement value, the lower the risk premium they require, and the more they pay Calmly per claim. Better data → higher pool operator prices → better claimant deals → more volume → better data. This is the flywheel that makes Calmly's economics improve with scale, while a new entrant's economics worsen until they build equivalent data.

Model limitation: flywheel not in fin-sim

The EV accuracy improvement curve and its effect on pool operator pricing are not modelled in the current fin-sim (calmly-fin-sim-v3-2026-05-19.html). The fin-sim models a static per-claim economics structure. The flywheel benefit — improving pool operator margins over time — is an upside not captured. This is flagged as a fin-sim development requirement before Phase II capital is committed.

Section 3 — I: Intrinsic performance capability

The competitive moat is the ultimate expression of the intrinsic performance capability. The data flywheel creates a structural improvement mechanism: as volume grows, accuracy improves, pool operator margins tighten, claimant deals improve, volume grows further. Each of the four moat components (data flywheel, methodology, assessment org network, pool operator calibration) has its own compounding property. Together they create an improving architecture — the per-claim economics at year 5 are better than at year 1, not because of management intervention, but because of the architecture's inherent design. Section 4 — T: Theorem-driven design
Pass

Data flywheel — Arrow (1962) learning-by-doing

Arrow's learning-by-doing is one of the foundational results in economic theory — Nobel Prize 2013 (Fama, Hansen, Shiller; Arrow's contributions well-established earlier). Applied extensively in manufacturing, services, and information economics. The EV accuracy improvement with cumulative claims is a direct application. Pass.

Network effects — Metcalfe (1980)

Network effects in claim data: as the dataset grows, the marginal value of each additional claim increases (better calibration, more defendant response patterns, tighter confidence intervals). Metcalfe's law is a heuristic rather than a theorem, but the underlying dynamic — value increasing with node count — is robust in data-driven platforms. Pass.

Complementary assets — Teece (1986)

Teece's complementary assets framework establishes that innovators capture value when they control complementary assets (data, relationships, methodology) that are co-specialised with the innovation. Calmly's four moat components are co-specialised — none is valuable without the others, and together they are extremely difficult to replicate. Teece (1986) is a foundational result in technology management and innovation strategy. Pass.
