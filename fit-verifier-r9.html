Section 1 — Strategic design: R9 Supplier Leverage

Supplier leverage diagnosis

Calmly's key external dependencies and their leverage risk:

1. Independent assessment org (primary risk): The entire architecture depends on this relationship. If the university withdraws, raises fees substantially, or is acquired by a competitor, the core mechanism fails. This is the single-point-of-failure in the C3 architecture.

2. Pool operators (law firms) (secondary risk): They buy claims. If they consolidate, collude on pricing, or exit the market, Calmly's revenue is at risk. Mitigated by exchange model structure — Calmly controls pool allocation.

3. Gateway partners (tertiary risk): They control consumer access. Concentration risk managed by 30% inflow limit per partner (R7 condition).

Primary risk to address at R9: assessment org single-source dependency.

Theory of Change — Lock-out (CMO format)

Class of problem: Single-source dependency — Porter (1980) supplier power. One supplier controls a critical input. Transaction cost economics (Williamson 1985) — asset specificity creates hold-up problems when the supplier knows the buyer has no alternative.

Theory: Williamson (1985) transaction cost economics — reducing asset specificity (making the methodology replicable) eliminates hold-up risk. Baldwin & Clark (2000) modularity theory — modular architecture allows component substitution. Both are foundational theories in strategy and organisational economics.

Mechanism: Calmly owns the assessment methodology and quality standards, not the assessment orgs. Multiple university law departments are onboarded using the same playbook (methodology, training, QA framework). Assessment orgs compete for claim volume on quality metrics. This creates: (1) competition that keeps fees at minimum viable level, (2) redundancy (no single-point failure), (3) geographic/subject matter coverage.

Outcome: No single assessment org has leverage over Calmly because the methodology is replicable. Assessment org fees compete downward at scale. Calmly's cost floor for the assessment layer (£16/claim) is a ceiling, not a floor — competition is expected to reduce it.

Pool operator and gateway partner leverage management

Pool operators: The exchange model itself manages leverage. Multiple pool operators compete for claim pools. Calmly controls the allocation mechanism — no single pool operator can be granted monopoly access. Pool operators' pricing models are calibrated to Calmly's EV accuracy (R10 data flywheel) — switching away from Calmly requires recalibrating their entire pricing framework.

Gateway partners: The 30% concentration limit (R7) ensures no single partner controls inflow. Organic channel maintained as backstop.

Cross-requirement note: R9 as potential cost upside

The multi-university competition model creates a potential downside on the assessment org fee. If multiple assessment orgs compete on quality and price, the £16/claim assessment fee may fall to £10–12/claim at scale. This is not assumed in the model (conservative) but is flagged as an upside scenario: if it materialises, the cost floor falls further and FMOS improves above 66%. Flagged for fin-sim sensitivity analysis.

Section 3 — I: Intrinsic performance capability
Pass

Methodology ownership creates a compounding competitive advantage in supplier management. Each additional assessment org onboarded: (1) increases the methodology's track record and credibility (making it easier to onboard further orgs), (2) adds geographic or subject matter coverage (raising service quality), and (3) strengthens Calmly's negotiating position with existing orgs (credible alternatives exist). The leverage elimination mechanism strengthens over time without additional investment. Pass.

Section 4 — T: Theorem-driven design
Pass

Supplier leverage — Porter (1980) five forces

Porter's supplier power analysis identifies single-source dependency as the primary lever for supplier bargaining power. The strategy — multiple substitutable suppliers competing on quality — is the canonical Porter response to high supplier concentration. Well-replicated in strategic management literature. Pass.

Asset specificity — Williamson (1985) TCE

Transaction cost economics predicts that asset specificity (a unique relationship with one supplier) creates hold-up risk. Reducing specificity (making the methodology portable) directly addresses this. Williamson (1985) is foundational in organisational economics — Nobel Prize 2009. Applied extensively in outsourcing, supply chain, and platform design literature. Pass.

Modularity — Baldwin & Clark (2000)

Modular architecture (Calmly owns the interface specification, assessment orgs implement it) enables component substitution without disrupting the whole system. Baldwin & Clark's modularity framework is directly applicable to multi-supplier platform design. Pass.

Verdict — R9 gate · 19 May 2026
PASS

FMOS: 66% — unchanged from R7–R8. R9 adds no new per-unit cost. Methodology ownership eliminates single-source dependency at zero cost — it is a structural design property.

Pool operator and gateway partner leverage are both managed through the exchange model structure (competitive allocation) and channel concentration limits respectively.

Section 6 — Conditions before capital commitment
  1. Condition 1Document the assessment org methodology as a replicable playbook. The methodology must be codified before the second assessment org is onboarded. Format: training manual + QA framework + interface specification (evidence categories, EV calculation methodology, output format). This is a product/operations deliverable, not a legal one — the IP protection comes from operational know-how, not patents.
  2. Condition 2Target at least three assessment orgs by end of ramp stage (18 months). Minimum viable redundancy: three orgs across different geographic jurisdictions or claim type specialisations. This ensures that no single withdrawal can halt operations.
  3. Condition 3Monitor assessment org fee sensitivity. If multi-org competition reduces the £16 assessment fee below £12/claim, update the fin-sim to reflect the cost saving. Track in quarterly cost reviews.
