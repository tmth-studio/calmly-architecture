Calmly — IVE Design Memo v2.0 — Exchange Architecture

Are we building the right thing?

A challenge-by-challenge audit of the Business Architecture Logic Model
Tom Manuel 18 May 2026 Framework: Integrative Venture Engineering (Simanis / Cornell) Supersedes: v1.0 (12 May 2026)
Architecture change — v1.0 → v2.0 · 18 May 2026

Discontinuous shift: from claim purchaser to litigation risk exchange

v1.0 modelled Calmly as a claims pooler that purchases consumer disputes outright, bears litigation risk on its own balance sheet, and earns revenue through the settlement spread. v2.0 reframes Calmly as a litigation risk exchange — infrastructure through which third-party investors purchase claims, earn the spread, and bear the litigation risk. Calmly operates the exchange and earns platform fees on each transaction.

This change is discontinuous. It affects the cost structure, the revenue model, the working capital position, the gateway partner architecture, and the definition of Calmly's customers. The core value proposition for claimants and defendants is unchanged; what changes is who bears the risk and how Calmly captures value.

  • C1 updated: Workaround Strategy and BFF statements revised — from "claims pooler that buys" to "exchange that facilitates investor purchase."
  • C3 updated: BFF statement revised to reflect capital-light exchange model.
  • C7 fully rewritten: Gateway partner confirmed as ITPs (not solicitors) for the exchange model. ITPs now perform a two-sided function — claimant origination and defendant notification — making them part of the exchange's operating infrastructure, not merely a referral channel. Integration ToC updated to Morgan & Hunt (1994) commitment-trust theory.
  • Two customers explicitly named: Claimants (supply side — sell claims to the exchange) and defendants (activated through ITP notification mechanism). The exchange serves both.
  • C2, C4, C5, C6, C8, C9, C10: Unchanged. The "market in litigation risk" framing in C4–C6 BFF statements is exchange-compatible and requires no revision.
Governing thought

The exchange architecture resolves the working capital constraint structurally — Calmly facilitates the market rather than bearing risk on its own balance sheet — and introduces two explicit customers (claimants and defendants) with ITPs serving as two-sided originators on both sides. The open design questions shift from C3 (working capital ToC, now resolved by the exchange model) to C7 (exchange originator activation) and C8 (TP switching cost).

The Business Architecture Logic Model poses ten universal functional requirements that any market-creating venture must satisfy, irrespective of industry or technology. This memo walks through each requirement, shows the current answer for every named sub-requirement, and flags what is resolved, partial, or still open. The goal is not to assess market size — that analysis sits in the pursuit memo and FMOS simulation. The goal is to check whether the design is internally coherent.

Architecture in view: the litigation risk exchange model (v2.0, 18 May 2026). The earlier risk-pooling / claims-purchase architecture (v1.0, 12 May 2026) is preserved at calmly-design-memo.html.

SR1
Conventional Business Operating System
The 'gold standard' way the core functionality or a commercial solution to a societal problem would typically be productised, made, sold, and delivered.
 Mediator. If someone has a dispute, they would first attempt to resolve it directly. If that fails, a mediator helps both parties reach a settlement — a process anchored to each side's BATNA (best alternative to a negotiated agreement). The settlement emerges from what each party would rationally accept given what they could achieve without agreement. ✓
SR2
Critical Limiting Operation
A key operation in the conventional business operating system that disproportionately drives up cost and creates a cost threshold that exceeds target customers' ability or willingness to pay.
 Creating a credible litigation backstop — the operation that gives mediation its leverage over the defendant. In markets where legal costs are recoverable, a solicitor performs this operation: they advise the claimant, serve notice, and make the threat credible by being professionally involved. The defendant participates in mediation because non-participation leads somewhere costly.

In the small claims market, no solicitor will take the case because there are no recoverable costs to fund them. The operation cannot be performed — not because it is too expensive, but because the structural precondition for it to exist is absent. The mediation BFF does not become uneconomic; it becomes structurally inoperative. The defendant can refuse to engage and face no consequences.

Zone: Selling the product.		 ✓
SR3
Workaround Theory of Change
The class of problem the critical limiting operation represents/falls into, and the theory/ies most effective in explaining how to solve that class of problem.
 Class of problem: Credible commitment failure. The claimant cannot credibly commit to litigation — individual claim economics make following through irrational. The defendant reads this correctly. Non-participation is their dominant strategy, not a misperception.

Theory: Schelling, T.C. (1960). The Strategy of Conflict. Harvard University Press. A threat changes behaviour only when the threatening party would actually follow through. Credibility is not about intent — it is about payoff structure. A party whose payoff makes following through rational is credible by design. Precommitment manufactures this credibility: by changing its own payoff structure, a party makes following through the dominant strategy and removes the option to back down.

Context: Defendants in the small claims market correctly assess that individual claimants cannot afford court. Costs are not recoverable at county court level. No solicitor will take the case. The claimant's BATNA is zero. Mediation is structurally inoperative — the defendant can decline and face no consequences.

Mechanism: A single claim presents a binary outcome: the claimant wins or loses, and that variance makes pursuit irrational even when EV is positive — the full downside falls on them alone. A portfolio investor holds a different problem. Across hundreds of claims, individual binary outcomes average out. The investor experiences the portfolio's expected value — a predictable return at known win rates and average claim values. Pursuit is rational not because it is cheap, but because they are making an EV decision, not a single bet. The investor's payoff makes litigating every qualifying claim the dominant strategy. That is the precommitment device. The defendant's position changes not because they are persuaded — but because the party they now face experiences litigation as an EV decision and has no rational reason to back down.

Outcome: Defendants face a credible litigation threat in the small claims market for the first time. Non-participation is no longer rational. The mediation BFF becomes operative — defendants face a genuine choice between settlement and court, and settlement is the rational response when the claim has merit. 		✓
SR4
Workaround Strategy
The best way to operationalize the Workaround TOC to eliminate the need for the Critical Limiting Operation in the core business operating system.
 Pool claimants' claims together, splitting the costs and revenues of litigation across the pool. At individual claim level, variance makes pursuit irrational even when EV is positive — the full downside falls on one person. At pool level, individual outcomes average out: pursuit becomes the rational committed strategy for whoever operates the pool.
v2.0 update: v1.0 stated "A third-party investor purchases rights to the claim." This revision makes two things explicit: (1) Calmly facilitates investor purchase, it does not make the purchase itself; (2) claims are pooled into investor-held portfolios — pooling is what makes the litigation threat credible, and the pool is owned by the investor vehicle, not Calmly.

High-Import Outcome & Attendant Routines
The outcome of greatest importance to target customers, and what they currently do, buy, think, and feel to achieve it.
 HIO (shared): We reach a settlement that reflects the merits of the case.
Claimant perspective: I receive what I am owed. Defendant perspective: I pay what I legitimately owe — no more.

Research clusters:

THINK: Claimant — "Is this worth pursuing? I don't know what I'm actually owed or how to make a credible demand." Defendant — "They won't go to court. And even if they do, I don't know what I actually owe, so my best move is to wait."

SR2
Key Monetizable Cost
The single greatest monetizable cost — in money, fear, or stress — that the solution eliminates. One cost per customer, but here the same underlying gap drives both.
 The cost of not knowing expected settlement value — for both parties.

Neither party can independently assess what the claim is worth. Every valuation each party holds is self-serving. No independent, outside-view assessment exists.

For the claimant: without a credible, quantified expected value, the claimant cannot make a demand the defendant must take seriously. They settle below EV or incur avoidable escalation costs pursuing a claim without a factual anchor.

For the defendant: without an independent assessment, the defendant faces an open-ended, unknown liability. They cannot distinguish a fair claim from an inflated one. This drives two equally costly responses: over-settling (paying more than owed to escape unknown exposure) or stonewalling (delaying until forced to pay more, with accumulated costs added). 		✓
SR3
Efficacy Theory of Change
The class of problem the key cost represents, and the theory most effective in explaining how to solve it.
 Class: Information asymmetry about expected settlement value, compounded by self-serving assessment. Neither party has access to an independent, outside-view assessment of what the claim is worth. Both form internally consistent but self-serving views. The gap between the two assessments is the direct predictor of failure to settle — not the underlying facts of the dispute.

Theory:
Babcock, L. & Loewenstein, G. (1997). Explaining bargaining impasse: The role of self-serving biases. Journal of Economic Perspectives, 11(1), 109–126. Self-serving assessment of legal merit is the single strongest predictor of litigation rates. The gap between each party's self-assessed win probability directly determines whether settlement occurs.

Kahneman, D. & Lovallo, D. (1993). Timid choices and bold forecasts: A cognitive perspective on risk taking. Management Science, 39(1), 17–31. Inside-view reasoning systematically overestimates individual success probability. Outside-view reference class data — what actually happens in cases like this — corrects it.

Kahneman, D. & Tversky, A. (1979). Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision under Risk. Econometrica, 47(2), 263–291. A quantified, escalating settlement ask frames delay as active loss accrual rather than a neutral deferral. Losses loom approximately twice as large as equivalent gains — making the cost of delay salient in a way a static settlement demand does not.

Context: Both parties have formed self-serving assessments of case value. Claimant overestimates their position; defendant underestimates their liability. Each assessment is internally consistent given the information each party holds. No independent arbiter exists. Settlement is blocked by the assessment gap, not by the facts of the dispute.

Mechanism: A quantitative expected value report provides both parties with the same outside-view assessment simultaneously — collapsing the self-serving assessment gap with reference class data. The report also calculates an escalation schedule: what the total settlement ask becomes at each stage of legal escalation as claimant costs (court fees, time, legal support) accumulate. The defendant can see precisely what early settlement costs versus what each subsequent stage costs. The cost of delay is no longer abstract — it is quantified and dated.

Outcome: Both parties hold the same factual basis for settlement. The self-serving assessment gap narrows because neither party can rationally dismiss a number produced by an outside-view methodology. The escalation schedule makes delay visibly and quantifiably irrational for the defendant. Early settlement becomes the rational response for claims with genuine merit. 		✓
SR4
Efficacy Strategy
The best way to operationalise the Efficacy Theory of Change to achieve a meaningful reduction in the key cost.
 Produce an expected value settlement report for each claim. The report has two components:

1. Expected settlement value. An outside-view, reference-class-based assessment of what the claim is worth at trial — stated as a single number with a methodology the defendant can scrutinise. This gives the claimant a factual basis for their demand and gives the defendant the independent assessment they need to make a rational decision about what they legitimately owe.

2. Escalation schedule. A quantified table showing what the settlement ask becomes at each stage of legal escalation — pre-court, post-issue, post-allocation, day of trial — as claimant costs (court fees, legal support, time) accumulate at each stage. The schedule makes the cost of delay explicit, dated, and arithmetic. The defendant is not told to settle — they are shown what each stage costs them relative to settling now.

The same report is disclosed to both parties simultaneously. The claimant sees what they are legitimately owed and what their ask becomes at each stage. The defendant sees exactly what early settlement costs compared to each subsequent stage. Without-prejudice framing applies: the settlement offer cannot be used as evidence of admission in other proceedings. 		✓

SR1
Critical Limiting Operation II
The operational procedure that commands the greatest amount of working capital as the venture scales — commands significant upfront investment and time before it starts to pay for itself.
 Paying the independent assessment organisation to produce settlement reports before revenues are generated from disputants. For every claim processed, Calmly pays £16.28 to the assessment org at the point of report production. Recovery is contingent on defendant settlement behaviour: settling claims recover in ~45 days; court-bound claims in ~365 days. This makes assessment org payments the only working capital line whose size is determined by an external variable — the defendant settlement rate — that Calmly can structurally influence through architectural design.

At Y5 scale (350,000 claims/year, 70% settling pre-trial), assessment org payments create a peak WC of £2.2M outstanding — second largest of all platform operations by absolute amount, and the only one with contingent rather than fixed-cycle recovery. Every other WC line (payroll, technology, facilities) resolves on a fixed contractual schedule. Assessment org payments do not. Drop the settlement rate from 70% to 50% and the WC rises from £2.2M to £3.2M — a £1M swing from a single architectural variable.

The working capital requirement is preceded by a one-time capital investment of ~£80k to stand up the independent assessment org before any claims flow: recruiting law students from university law programmes, developing and publishing the EV methodology, and establishing university partnerships. This investment must be made before the org has any track record — and therefore before its outputs have any effect on defendant settlement behaviour.
v2.0: Replaces v1.0 CLO II (13-month claim financing lag). In v2.0, Calmly does not hold claims — investors do. The working capital constraint shifts from claim financing to assessment org credibility-building.

SR2
Scaling Theory of Change
The class of problem the CLO II represents, and the theory most effective in explaining how to solve it. Uses CMO structure (Pawson & Tilley, 1997): class of problem, theory, context, mechanism, outcome.
 Class of problem: Recovery timing — a maturity mismatch between a fixed cost commitment (report production, £16.28/claim) and a variable recovery period whose length is determined by defendant behaviour within mediation. The WC outstanding is not a function of whether defendants settle but of how quickly they do: settling claims recover in ~45 days; court-bound claims in ~365 days. An 8× difference in cycle length from a single architectural variable.

Theory: Spence (1973) — signalling theory, applied specifically to the speed dimension of recipient response. A signal from a party with no financial stake eliminates the credibility objection that defendants use to extend mediation timelines. A defendant who cannot dispute the report's independence on structural grounds has no tactical basis for delay — the stalling strategy loses its factual justification.

Context: Coming out of C2, the mediation process begins. Even defendants who accept that settlement is inevitable can extend the timeline by disputing the report's objectivity — "Calmly has a financial interest in the settlement amount." This is a legitimate observation, not motivated reasoning (C2's problem). It extends recovery periods unnecessarily, inflating the WC float.

Mechanism: The report source switches from Calmly to an independent organisation with no financial stake. The defendant's credibility objection — the primary tactical basis for delay within mediation — loses its structural foundation. Defendants cannot extend mediation on source-credibility grounds. The cash cycle shortens.

Outcome: Average recovery time on assessment org payments shortens → WC outstanding on the CLO II contracts.
v2.0 update: Class of problem corrected — C3 is a recovery timing problem, not a source credibility problem. Source credibility is the mechanism that solves it. "Settlement rate rises" removed from outcome — that is C2's metric. C3's metric is recovery time (how quickly defendants settle within mediation, not whether they settle).
 ✓
SR3
Scaling Strategy
The best way to operationalise the Scaling ToC to circumvent or eliminate the CLO II. Must transform the working capital constraint — not transfer it to a third party. Cost-shifting is not a Scaling Strategy.
 Establish an independent assessment organisation — structurally separate from Calmly, staffed by law students sourced from university law programmes, operating under no financial stake in any claim outcome — to generate every EV settlement report. The independence is the mechanism: because the org cannot benefit from inflating case EV, defendants have no structural basis to apply a motivated-source discount. The Scaling Strategy is not a financing arrangement. It is an architectural decision about report authorship that converts defendant scepticism — the root cause of the WC constraint — into a design variable Calmly controls.

Cost-shifting test: this is not cost-shifting. The working capital does not move to the assessment org or to a third party. It shrinks — because the mechanism that creates it (low settlement rate from defendant scepticism) is structurally reduced by the independence of the source.
v2.0: Replaces v1.0 SR3 (rating organisation + investor vehicle + double-signal mechanism). The investor vehicle is removed — in v2.0 Calmly does not need investors to create a credibility signal. Source independence alone is sufficient.

SR1
Target Customer's Implicit Efficacy Theory of Change
Target customers' current understanding or belief as to the underlying nature of the problem presented by the High-Import Outcome, and therefore the main determinant of success in achieving it.
 Underlying nature of the problem (entering C4, given C1 and C3 are in place): The consumer's claim has been independently validated (C3) and they are in or considering joining a pool of similar claims (C1). The question is no longer whether the claim is valid. The consumer's belief about what determines the outcome is: litigation capability. The stronger the collective legal case — quality of evidence, strength of argument, credibility of legal backing — the better the settlement. A weak case, even a valid one, gets a low offer or nothing. A strong, well-prepared case gets the defendant to settle at fair value.

The implicit theory of change: "The better our litigation capability, the better the settlement I will get."

Main determinant of success: The disputants' perception of the claimants' ability to convert a theoretically strong case into an enforced judgment. C3 established that the legal argument is sound — both parties now know that. What neither party is certain about is whether the claimants can follow the argument all the way through: to court, to a judgment, and through enforcement. The defendant's settlement decision reflects their belief about whether the claimants will fold at any of those steps. The settlement premium paid is the price of that uncertainty.

Implication for the Want Block: C4 cannot rely on further validation of the legal argument — C3 already provided that. It needs to change the defendant's (and claimant's) belief about the claimants' capacity to execute through to enforcement. That is the capability gap the attraction strategy must close. The category entry point is when the free EV assessment returns: argument confirmed, execution capacity still in question — the decision is whether to proceed or absorb the loss. 		✓
SR2
Key Efficacy Doubt
The factor that plays the most significant role in causing targeted customers to implicitly or explicitly doubt the need for the offering or doubt that it works.
 Key Efficacy Doubt (pool financial survivability): Claimants pool upfront costs and share revenues. Early in the portfolio, losses are drawn down before the expected value materialises. Each claimant watches their contribution being consumed by early losses and faces a rational temptation to exit — cutting personal losses before the pool depletes further. Some crack. The pool shrinks. The collective legal threat becomes less credible as it loses members. The defendant's rational response is to wait — knowing that the pool is under financial and psychological pressure to unwind before enforcement. The implicit doubt: "Even if the expected value is real, will the pool survive long enough, financially and psychologically, for it to materialise?"

Road not taken — execution credibility: whether the pool has the legal capability to follow through to enforcement. Plausible but secondary — the pool has law student support and an independent EV assessment. The more structural threat to the model is financial survivability under early losses, not legal capability per se.		 ✓
SR3
Attraction Theory of Change
The nature of the customer's Key Doubt and the theory/ies most effective in explaining how to solve that type of challenge.
 Class of problem: War of attrition / coordination failure under sequential losses. A shared-cost pool creates an individual incentive to exit early — each claimant watching their upfront contribution drawn down by early losses faces a rational temptation to cut losses and leave, even though collective persistence is the optimal strategy for the pool as a whole. The pool is vulnerable to sequential exits that erode the collective threat before the expected value has materialised. The same dynamic that causes strikes to collapse.

Theory: Olson (1965) — collective action fails when individually rational behaviour undermines collective benefit; members defect when the personal cost of continued participation exceeds the perceived share of collective gain. Arrow (1963) — insurance resolves the individual incentive to defect by transferring idiosyncratic downside risk to a risk-pooling mechanism, making persistence individually rational regardless of the pool's current loss position.

Context: Claimant who has joined the pool and contributed upfront cash. Early claims are lost. Shared cash is drawn down. The doubt is not whether the expected value is real — the independent assessment confirmed that — but whether the pool will survive long enough, financially and psychologically, for it to materialise.

Mechanism: Pool losses are insured. Individual claimants' contributions are protected against early losses. No individual faces the financial incentive to exit early because their downside is covered. The collective threat remains credible throughout the portfolio's natural loss distribution. Persistence becomes individually rational — the strike holds.

Outcome: Individual exit incentive is eliminated. The pool persists through early losses. The collective legal threat remains credible to defendants throughout the prosecution cycle.		 ✓
SR4
Attraction Strategy
The best way to operationalize the Attraction Theory of Change for getting around or eliminating entirely the customer's Key Efficacy Doubt.
 Strategy: Offer insurance as a structural feature of pool membership — not an add-on, but the mechanism that makes collective persistence individually rational from the moment of joining.

The doubt identified in SR2 is financial: will the pool survive long enough under early losses for the expected value to materialise? Insurance resolves this by decoupling individual financial exposure from the pool's short-term loss position. Claimants join knowing their upfront contribution is protected against early losses. The pool absorbs the natural loss distribution of a litigation portfolio without the coordination failure that would otherwise cause sequential exits and collapse the collective threat.

Flow: Consumer decides to join the pool → pool membership includes loss insurance as standard → claimant contributions are protected regardless of early claim outcomes → pool persists through the natural loss distribution → expected value materialises for all members.

Entry point: Organic SEO at the moment of final refusal — peak receptivity, before adaptation sets in. The insurance feature is disclosed at sign-up: the claimant knows from the outset that their contribution is protected.		 ✓

Required Product Use Routine
The steps involved in using the interim offering and where they interface with the customer's life or organisational context (in terms of time, place, relationships, money, and identity).
  1. Consumer discovers Calmly at category entry point — organic search post-final-refusal (C7 origination)

  7. Consumer receives settlement notification and payment from pool recovery, minus agreed fee (passive)
Interface with consumer life: Time — one concentrated submission session at step 2; occasional passive updates thereafter. Place — fully digital; no court attendance. Relationships — none required (pool operator manages defendant contact). Money — no upfront payment; contingent fee from recovery. Identity — from frustrated individual claimant to pool participant with passive claim management.

SR2
Key Learning Block (Use Block)
The biggest disruption or barrier preventing the target customer from learning the new product use routine.
 Primary block — anticipated litigation complexity (steps 5–6 transition): The consumer who has formed the intention to join the pool (C4 resolved) carries a mental model of what being a party to litigation requires — court hearings to attend, legal documents to review, CPR procedures to comply with, tactical decisions to make. This expectation of sustained specialist-grade effort is the Use Block. It manifests at the point of sign-up and post-sign-up: consumers either hesitate to commit (expecting ongoing demands) or, once in the pool, disengage in anticipation of complexity that never materialises. The structural source is the conventional BFF for dispute resolution, where a "litigant in person" manages their own case. That mental model is accurate for unrepresented court proceedings — but does not describe Calmly's architecture, where the pool operator manages all proceedings.

Secondary block — evidence assembly anxiety (step 2): At the initial submission step, the consumer does not know what evidence is sufficient and fears submitting an incomplete case. This is a bounded, one-time barrier — not the primary block, but a real activation friction point.

Road not taken — evidence assembly as primary block: The prior C5 architecture treated step 2 (evidence submission) as the primary use block, addressed with a guided submission flow (Bandura self-efficacy). That analysis is correct for the submission step but misidentifies the structural source of the Use Block. The deeper barrier is anticipated ongoing litigation complexity — a structural mismatch between consumer expectations (manage litigation) and product design (pool operator manages everything).		 ✓
SR3
Adoption Theory of Change
The class of problem the Learning Block represents and the theory/ies most effective in explaining how to solve it.
 Class of problem: Transaction cost mismatch — lay claimants bear prohibitively high transaction costs managing CPR-compliant litigation; the pool operator (professional litigant) bears low transaction costs managing the same activity at scale.

Theory: Coase (1937), "The Nature of the Firm" — economic activities are organised by the party that can execute them at lowest total cost. Williamson (1985) — asset specificity: when a party has investments that are highly specific to a transaction (legal expertise, established court relationships, CPR-compliant templates), organising the activity within that party's operation rather than requiring the other party to replicate those assets is structurally efficient.

Amortisation Strategy
The best way to operationalize the Amortisation Theory of Change to eliminate the cash flow constraint.
 Claimant: contingent fee deducted from recovery proceeds. No payment is due at sign-up, during the pool period, or at any pre-recovery stage. Calmly charges a platform fee defined as a percentage of the gross settlement amount received from the defendant, deducted at the point of settlement before proceeds are distributed to the claimant. The payment event is architecturally inseparable from the value delivery event — they are the same transaction. This is not a cash flow accommodation: it is an architecture where no cash flow constraint can exist. The required fee rate is determined by the financial model (cost floor ÷ (1 − FMOS target) ÷ average settlement); see FIT Verifier R6.

Defendant: a defendant financing app bridges the timing gap. Where a defendant faces a genuine liquidity constraint, a verified financing app on the Calmly marketplace pays the pool the full settlement amount on day one — the claimant receives net recovery immediately. The defendant repays the financing app in instalments over an agreed term. This avoids the zero-sum structure of pool-operator instalment terms (where the claimant waits for the defendant to pay) by inserting a specialist credit intermediary who absorbs the timing mismatch and prices the credit risk. The pool operator's P&L is clean; Calmly's platform fee is earned at settlement day regardless of the defendant's repayment schedule.

Why a separate app, not a pool operator obligation: the pool operator's function is litigation management, not credit provision. Forcing instalment terms onto the pool operator creates credit risk exposure incompatible with their role and creates a zero-sum timing problem for the claimant. A financing app is a specialist who can diversify across many defendant liabilities, price the risk actuarially, and offer lower effective rates than a non-specialist lender.

What makes defendant financing possible: not the EV assessment but the signed settlement agreement. Once the defendant has agreed to settle for a specific sum, the financing app holds a legally binding obligation for a known, fixed amount — purpose-specific credit where the lender knows exactly what the funds are for, where they go, and that the borrower cannot redirect them. Purpose-specific lending attracts lower rates than general consumer credit because it eliminates adverse selection risk (Stiglitz & Weiss, 1981 — information asymmetry is the primary driver of credit rationing; purpose-specific lending collapses that asymmetry). The EV assessment's role ends at D2 — it brings the defendant to the settlement decision. The financing app's underwriting is against the signed agreement and the defendant's credit profile, not the EV number.

R3 interaction: no change to Calmly's working capital model. Claimant payment arrives at recovery (already modelled). Financing app operates on its own P&L — Calmly earns a platform fee for app curation, not a share of the credit spread. 		✓

Lock-In Theory of Change
The parameter on which the Key Store of Value rests and the theory/ies most effective in explaining how to solve for that parameter.
 Class of problem: Relational and informational assets that are specific to the partnership, generated by the relationship over time, and non-transferable to a new provider.

Theory: Burnham, Frels & Mahajan (2003) switching cost taxonomy. Three categories of switching cost accumulate simultaneously: procedural (retraining staff, updating materials, revising policy documentation — manageable but real and time-consuming); financial (loss of the accumulated outcome data that underpins the ITP's welfare reporting — moderate, growing over time); relational (loss of community-level reputation and member trust built around the exchange within that ITP's specific population — the most durable of the three; cannot be purchased or replicated quickly).

Context: An ITP that has worked with the exchange for two years has produced two annual impact reports showing welfare outcomes for its members on both sides of the market, trained its frontline staff to refer disputes as standard practice, and built a reputation among its members as "the organisation that can actually help with disputes — and holds defendants to account." Each of these assets took time to build and is specific to the exchange relationship.

Mechanism: Switching to a competitor requires rebuilding all three categories simultaneously. The procedural cost is quantifiable and can in theory be funded. The financial cost — losing historical outcome data — grows with each year of the relationship and is never portable in a form a new provider can immediately use. The relational cost is the most structural: the community reputation within the ITP's population is a function of demonstrated outcomes over time in that community specifically, and a new provider starts unknown. The combined switching cost rises nonlinearly with tenure.

Outcome: ITPs do not switch to a competitor even when a marginally better offer arrives, because the total switching cost — procedural, financial, and relational — exceeds the marginal benefit of switching for most plausible competitive offers.
⚠ Open: Theory is drafted but not formally validated against the IVE CMO structure. The mechanism field — how ITPs reason through the switching cost when a competitor approaches — needs tightening. Outcome needs quantification: what switching cost magnitude is sufficient?

SR3
Lock-In Strategy
The best way to operationalize the Lock-In Theory of Change to accrete value that the customer risks losing upon switching to a competitor.
 Four active mechanisms, in order of structural importance:

1. Outcome reporting. Produce regular impact reports per ITP — quarterly summary, annual deep report — formatted for the ITP's own board and funder communications. Use the ITP's mission language and stakeholder framing. Include both claimant outcomes and defendant notification settlement rates within the ITP's ecosystem. The more embedded the exchange's data is in the ITP's institutional narrative, the higher the financial switching cost. The goal is for the ITP's board to cite exchange outcomes in its own annual report — at which point the relationship is load-bearing in the ITP's institutional identity.

2. Integration depth. Embed referral and notification pathways in the ITP's own materials and procedures at onboarding — staff training script, member welcome pack paragraph, grievance policy reference, defendant notification template, website link. Offer to draft these materials for the ITP. The deeper the integration at the start, the higher the procedural switching cost from day one.

3. Exclusivity agreements. Preferred or exclusive provider agreements with key ITPs before competitors enter the segment. The contractual complement to the relational switching cost — it makes switching require active contract breach, not just a decision. What the exchange offers in exchange: dedicated account management, co-branded member communications, enhanced impact reporting, joint welfare credentialing, and defendant notification analytics. The execution architecture — what specifically goes in the exclusivity agreement and what the exchange provides in exchange — is the outstanding design task.

4. Community reputation building. Use outcome data (with consent) to produce member-facing case studies within each ITP's own communications channels. Members who see demonstrated outcomes in their own community become active advocates — they ask for the exchange by name when disputes arise, creating a demand-pull that sits outside the ITP's institutional decision. This is the most durable layer of lock-in.
⚠ Open: Exclusivity agreement architecture not yet designed. Sequencing unclear: at what point in the ITP relationship is exclusivity proposed — at onboarding, after first year, after outcome data exists?

Lock-Out Theory of Change
The parameter on which the leverage risk rests and the theory/ies most effective in explaining how to eliminate or circumvent it.
 Class of problem: Single-source dependency — Porter (1980) supplier power. One supplier controls a critical input. Transaction cost economics (Williamson 1985) — asset specificity creates hold-up problems when the supplier knows the buyer has no alternative.

Theory: Williamson (1985) transaction cost economics — reducing asset specificity (making the methodology replicable) eliminates hold-up risk. Baldwin & Clark (2000) modularity theory — modular architecture allows component substitution. Both are foundational theories in strategy and organisational economics.

Mechanism: Calmly owns the assessment methodology and quality standards, not the assessment orgs. Multiple university law departments are onboarded using the same playbook (methodology, training, QA framework). Assessment orgs compete for claim volume on quality metrics. This creates: (1) competition that keeps fees at minimum viable level, (2) redundancy (no single-point failure), (3) geographic and subject matter coverage.

Outcome: No single assessment org has leverage over Calmly because the methodology is replicable. Assessment org fees compete downward at scale. The £16/claim assessment cost is a ceiling, not a floor — competition is expected to reduce it.		 ✓

Interim Value Chain / Key Competitive Asset
The key resource, data asset, or network effect that compounds over time to create a structural advantage that competitors cannot easily replicate — even when the idea itself is visible.
 Primary moat — data flywheel (compound accuracy advantage): Every resolved claim adds to Calmly's dataset of defendant response patterns, claim EV accuracy, and settlement timelines. Secondary moat — methodology ownership: The EV assessment methodology, quality standards, and training framework are Calmly's IP. Not patentable, but 2–3 years to calibrate against real case outcomes. A competitor must build this from scratch.

Tertiary moat — assessment org network: Each onboarded university law department has invested in training and calibration. They are sticky partners. A competitor must build this network simultaneously with building volume — a chicken-and-egg problem.

Quaternary moat — pool operator calibration: Pool operators have invested in understanding Calmly's portfolio quality. Their pricing models are calibrated to Calmly's EV accuracy. Switching requires recalibrating the entire pricing framework.		 ✓
SR2
Leverage Theory of Change
The parameter on which the competitive moat rests and the theory/ies most effective in explaining how it self-reinforces over time.
 Class of problem: Structural competitive advantage — creating barriers that prevent replication even when the idea is visible.

Theory: Metcalfe (1980) network effects — value grows with nodes (claim data, assessment org connections, pool operator relationships). Arrow (1962) learning-by-doing — EV assessment accuracy improves with cumulative claim volume (a manufacturing learning curve applied to an information product). Teece (1986) complementary assets — proprietary methodology + data + relationships are complementary assets that together create durable advantage; individually replicable, together extremely difficult to replicate simultaneously.

Mechanism: As claim volume grows, EV accuracy improves → pool operators price more accurately → Calmly can offer claimants better deals → more claimants choose Calmly → more volume → more accuracy. This positive feedback loop widens the moat over time.

Outcome: After 36+ months of at-scale operation, a new entrant cannot replicate Calmly's EV accuracy without historical data — and cannot build historical data without the volume that the accuracy advantage enables. The moat is self-reinforcing.		 ✓
SR3
Leverage Strategy
The best way to operationalize the Leverage Theory of Change to establish and widen the competitive moat at scale.
 Calmly's leverage at scale: data quality as a pricing advantage. Pool operators price claim pools based on EV accuracy — the tighter the confidence interval on expected settlement value, the lower the risk premium they require, and the more they pay per claim. Better data → higher pool operator prices → better claimant deals → more volume → better data. This flywheel makes Calmly's economics improve with scale, while a new entrant's economics worsen until they build equivalent data.

The moat in one line: Calmly's historical claim resolution data is the methodology that prices the market. You cannot build the market without the data. You cannot build the data without being in the market.		 ✓
