Section 1 — Strategic design: R4 Want Block

SR1 — Implicit Theory of Change (claimant)

Consumer's implicit ToC: "The main determinant of success is perceived ability to convert a theoretically strong case into an enforced judgment." C3 already established that the legal argument is sound via independent assessment. What neither party is certain about at R4 is whether the claimants can follow through: to court, to a judgment, through enforcement.

The question is not "is my case good?" but "can this group of people actually see it through?"

SR2 — Key Efficacy Doubt: pool financial survivability

The doubt: War of attrition / coordination failure under sequential losses. Claimants pool upfront costs and share revenues. Early in the portfolio, losses are drawn down before the expected value materialises. Each claimant watches their contribution being consumed by early losses and faces a rational temptation to exit.

The structural problem: Even if the expected value is real, individually rational early exit destroys collective value. Will the pool survive long enough — financially and psychologically — for the expected value to materialise?

SR3 — Theory of Change (Attraction)

Class of problem: War of attrition / coordination failure under sequential losses.

Theory 1 — Olson (1965): Collective action fails when individually rational behaviour undermines collective benefit. Each claimant has an individual incentive to exit early (protect their contribution) that, if acted on, destroys the pool's expected value for everyone.

Theory 2 — Arrow (1963): Insurance resolves the individual incentive to defect by transferring idiosyncratic downside risk to a risk-pooling mechanism. If pool losses are insured, individual claimants' contributions are protected against early losses. No individual faces the financial incentive to exit early because their downside is covered.

Mechanism: Pool loss insurance eliminates the rational basis for early exit. The pool can persist through the natural loss distribution because no member bears the cost of early losses individually.

SR4 — Attraction Strategy

Offer pool loss insurance as a structural feature of pool membership — not an add-on, but the mechanism that makes collective persistence individually rational from the moment of joining.

Decision sequence: Consumer decides to join the pool → pool membership includes insurance as standard → claimant contributions are protected regardless of early claim outcomes → pool persists through the natural loss distribution → expected value materialises for all members.

Channel: Organic SEO + word of mouth at the category entry point — consumers searching for help after receiving a company's final refusal. Peak receptivity. Mechanism unchanged from prior architecture; insurance is what makes joining individually rational, not how they find Calmly.

SR5 — Cumulative BFF (C1–C4)

A verified dispute resolution marketplace. The marketplace provides the infrastructure: claims pooling that creates a credible collective threat of legal action, a settlement mechanism through which disputants reach an optimal agreement, and payment rails from which Calmly earns fees per resolved claim (C1).

By C4, two verified apps run on the marketplace: an EV assessment app — the quantitative settlement report produced by an independent assessment organisation staffed by university law students with no financial stake in the outcome, disclosed simultaneously to both parties (C3); and a pool insurance app — protecting members' upfront contributions against early losses, eliminating individual incentive to exit before the expected value materialises (C4).

Section 3 — I: Intrinsic performance capability
Pass

Insurance intrinsic property: At scale, pool loss insurance is actuarially priced. The insurer absorbs volatility across a large portfolio of claims; individual pool members pay a premium calibrated to the expected loss distribution. This is a structural property of insurance markets — it does not depend on any individual claimant's psychology or persistence. Pool survival becomes mechanically guaranteed at scale, not contingent on human behaviour.

CAC dilution: Organic SEO amortisation is a fixed content investment — per-claim CAC falls as claims volume grows without additional spend. Word of mouth is zero marginal cost and grows with satisfied claimants. At 1,500 claims/month, WOM is expected to account for 40–50% of inflow, further reducing effective CAC below £38. Structural property, not operational target.

Pass.

Section 4 — T: Theorem-driven design
Pass

Attraction — Olson (1965) + Arrow (1963)

Olson (1965) — The Logic of Collective Action: Collective action fails when individually rational behaviour undermines collective benefit. The free-rider / early-exit problem in the claims pool is a direct instance of Olson's core claim. Well-established in political economy, public goods theory, and organisational behaviour. No novel application required — the problem is canonical.

Arrow (1963) — Uncertainty and the Welfare Economics of Medical Care: Insurance resolves the individual incentive to defect by transferring idiosyncratic downside risk to a risk-pooling mechanism. Arrow's foundational insight — that insurance markets exist precisely because individuals cannot bear idiosyncratic risk efficiently — underpins the structural fix. Applied here to pool loss insurance: individual claimants transfer the risk of early portfolio losses to an insurer; no individual bears the cost of the natural loss distribution; the rational basis for early exit disappears.

Both theorems are among the most replicated in economics. The application here is direct and non-exotic. Pass.

Reach — category entry point SEO (pass note)

The channel to find Calmly is unchanged from prior architecture: organic SEO at the category entry point — consumers searching after receiving a company's final refusal. Peak receptivity, transactional intent, well-documented in consumer fintech and legal tech (MoneySavingExpert, Resolver, Which?). No novel theory required — empirically grounded in established category practice. Pass.

Section 5 — Verdict
Verdict — R4 gate · 19 May 2026
PASS

FMOS: 63.2% — clears 25% F1 gate and 60% final gate. R4 adds two costs: CAC £38 + insurance curation £2 = £40 total addition. Cost floor moves from C3 £199 to R4 £239. Impact on FMOS: −0.4pp from C3. Immaterial.

The Attraction Strategy resolves the pool financial survivability doubt (SR2) using Olson (1965) and Arrow (1963). Pool loss insurance, offered as standard membership, eliminates the rational basis for early exit. Individual claimants' contributions are protected regardless of early claim outcomes. Pool persistence becomes structurally guaranteed at scale, not dependent on psychology or coordination. War of attrition is solved by design.

Insurance premium is paid by pool members, not by Calmly. Calmly earns a marketplace fee from the insurer. Calmly's only cost is oversight and curation of the insurer relationship (~£2/claim). The insurance mechanism is therefore revenue-generating for Calmly, not a cost sink.

Section 6 — Conditions before capital commitment
  1. Condition 1Validate insurance pricing. Tier 4 assumption: a specialist insurer can price pool loss insurance actuarially at premiums affordable to pool members. This has not been tested with an actual underwriter. Validate through: approach specialist legal expenses insurers (e.g. ARAG, DAS) before pool launch to confirm underwriting appetite, confirm that pool loss is a recognised insurable event, and obtain indicative premium range. If premium is unaffordable to pool members, the SR4 mechanism fails regardless of its theoretical elegance.
  2. Condition 2Validate insurance curation overhead (£2/claim). Tier 4 assumption. Actual cost depends on the complexity of managing the insurer relationship, claims processing, and policy administration. £2/claim is a reasonable starting estimate at 18,000 claims/yr but has not been grounded in operational experience. Refine during pilot using actual FTE time tracking on insurer relationship management.
  3. Condition 3Measure pool persistence under insurance. The core SR4 assumption is that insured pools have materially lower early-exit rates than uninsured pools. This is theoretically well-grounded (Arrow 1963) but empirically untested in this specific context. Pilot must measure: early-exit rate in insured pools (target <10%) against working hypothesis of ~30% without insurance. If insured early-exit rate does not materially outperform uninsured, the mechanism is not working as designed and the architecture requires revision before scale.
